# PIPA Privacy Policy Help

Last reviewed: 2026-04-21

This guide explains how a BC organization should think about a "PIPA policy"
warning, missing policy evidence, and privacy obligations when the organization
is small, complex, or does not have access to a full member list.

This is product and compliance guidance, not legal advice. Organizations should
get legal advice for high-risk data, disputes, bylaw conflicts, statutory
student-society questions, or a regulator complaint.

## Quick answer

British Columbia's Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA) does not simply
ask whether an organization has uploaded a file called "Privacy Policy."

PIPA requires an organization to:

- designate one or more people responsible for privacy compliance;
- develop and follow privacy policies and practices needed to comply with PIPA;
- develop a complaint process;
- make information about those policies, practices, and the complaint process
  available on request;
- collect, use, and disclose personal information only with consent or another
  authority under PIPA;
- give collection notices where required;
- protect personal information with reasonable safeguards;
- respond to access and correction requests; and
- retain or dispose of personal information according to PIPA's rules.

In Societyer, "No linked document" should be read as an evidence warning: the
workspace does not have a privacy policy document linked. It is not proof, by
itself, that the organization has no legally required privacy practices.

## What PIPA applies to

PIPA generally applies to private-sector organizations in BC, including
not-for-profits, charities, trade unions, corporations, partnerships, and
unincorporated associations that collect, use, or disclose personal information.

PIPA obligations are tied to personal information that the organization collects,
uses, discloses, stores, or controls. "Personal information" means information
about an identifiable individual. Examples can include member names, personal
emails, phone numbers, volunteer applications, staff records, event
registrations, reimbursement details, complaints, casework notes, election
records, and mailing list preferences.

Business contact information is treated differently from personal information in
some parts of PIPA, but organizations should still handle it carefully.

## What the policy should cover

A useful PIPA privacy policy or privacy program should cover:

- the types of personal information collected;
- why the information is collected;
- how the information is used;
- when and why information may be disclosed;
- how consent is obtained, tracked, withdrawn, or replaced by another legal
  authority;
- who can access personal information internally;
- what safeguards are used;
- how long records are kept;
- how records are securely deleted or anonymized;
- how individuals can request access to their personal information;
- how individuals can request corrections;
- how privacy complaints are handled;
- who the privacy officer or privacy contact is; and
- how service providers or contractors must protect personal information.

The policy does not need to be long. It needs to match what the organization
actually does.

## Small organizations

A small volunteer-run organization still needs a privacy baseline. PIPA does not
have a general exemption for organizations with a small budget, no staff, or only
a few directors.

A practical small-organization setup can be lightweight:

- one director, officer, or staff member is assigned as privacy officer;
- the privacy officer's title and contact information are available on request;
- the organization keeps a short privacy policy;
- forms explain why information is collected;
- personal information is stored in a controlled place, not scattered across
  personal inboxes and devices;
- only people who need the information can access it;
- old personal information is deleted when it is no longer needed;
- access requests and complaints are logged; and
- the board reviews the setup at least annually.

Example small-organization policy statement:

> The Society collects personal information only for society operations,
> membership, events, governance, volunteer administration, and legal compliance.
> Access is limited to authorized directors, officers, staff, and service
> providers. Individuals may contact the privacy officer to ask questions,
> request access or correction, or make a complaint.

## Larger or more complex organizations

A larger organization should use a more formal privacy management program. Size
is not the only factor. The level of detail should also reflect the sensitivity
and volume of personal information.

More structure is appropriate when the organization:

- has staff, contractors, or many volunteers;
- provides services to vulnerable people;
- handles casework, advocacy, counselling, discipline, complaints, grants,
  bursaries, payroll, health, disability, immigration, financial, or legal
  information;
- uses a CRM, helpdesk, voting platform, analytics tool, cloud drive, email
  platform, or donor system;
- shares data with partners, funders, parent bodies, or public institutions; or
- operates across more than one jurisdiction.

For these organizations, the privacy program should usually include:

- role-based access controls;
- written retention schedules;
- training for staff, directors, and volunteers;
- breach-response procedures;
- service-provider privacy terms;
- privacy review before adopting new systems;
- periodic access reviews;
- documented access/correction request workflow; and
- board or senior management oversight.

## When the organization does not know its members

Some societies, especially student societies or organizations connected to a
university or parent institution, may not receive a full member list. That does
not make PIPA disappear.

The right framing is:

> PIPA applies to the organization for personal information the organization
> collects, uses, discloses, stores, or controls. A university-held or
> parent-held member list is not treated as organization-controlled unless the
> organization has access to it, a copy of it, a contractual right to obtain it,
> a shared system, or the practical/legal ability to direct its use or
> disclosure.

If a university or public post-secondary institution holds the member or student
data independently, disclosure by that institution may be governed by public
sector privacy law or a specific statute. PIPA does not, by itself, force the
institution to disclose a list to the society.

The organization must still comply with PIPA for the personal information it
does have, such as:

- director and officer records;
- volunteer records;
- staff or contractor records;
- emails from members or students;
- event registrations;
- newsletter subscribers;
- election or referendum records the organization actually receives;
- grant, club funding, or reimbursement records;
- complaints and casework files;
- website form submissions; and
- records stored in the organization's cloud tools or service providers.

### Documenting the member-data gap

The organization should keep a short board-approved memo or privacy file that
records:

- the bylaw or statute that defines membership;
- what member records the organization is legally expected to maintain;
- what data was requested from the university or parent institution;
- why the data was requested;
- the date and content of any refusal, limitation, or condition;
- why the institution-held list is not under the organization's custody or
  control;
- what personal information the organization does control;
- how the organization will collect necessary information directly from members
  where lawful;
- how referendum-only or purpose-limited datasets will be separated; and
- when the issue will be reviewed again.

Template:

> The Society requested access to the institutional member list for the purpose
> of maintaining society records and communicating with members. As of
> [date], [institution] has not disclosed that list and has not provided the
> Society with access to or control over those records. The Society therefore
> treats the institutional list as outside the Society's custody and control for
> PIPA response purposes, while continuing to comply with PIPA for personal
> information the Society collects or controls.

For statutory referendum or fee-process data:

> Any student personal information received for a statutory referendum or fee
> process will be segregated, used only for that purpose, access-restricted, and
> deleted or retained only as required for legal, audit, or dispute-resolution
> purposes.

## FAQ

### Does PIPA require a public website privacy policy?

PIPA requires the organization to develop and follow privacy policies and
practices, have a complaint process, and make information about them available
on request. A website policy is often useful, but the key legal requirement is
not simply "publish a web page."

### Does "No linked document" mean the organization is non-compliant?

Not necessarily. It means Societyer does not have a document linked as evidence.
The organization might have privacy practices outside Societyer, or it might
need to create them. The safest workflow is to verify whether a policy exists,
upload or link it if it does, and create one if it does not.

### Does a tiny organization need a privacy officer?

Yes. PIPA requires one or more individuals to be responsible for compliance. In a
small organization, this can usually be a director, officer, staff member, or
other accountable person.

### Can the privacy officer be a role instead of a named person?

The organization should be able to provide a position name or title and contact
information. For public-facing documents, a role-based contact such as "Privacy
Officer" with a monitored email address is usually easier to maintain than a
personal email for a changing volunteer.

### We do not receive the university member list. Does PIPA apply?

Yes, for the information the organization collects or controls. The lack of a
university-provided member list may be a governance and records issue, but it is
not a PIPA exemption.

### Can we scrape, infer, or buy a substitute member list?

Usually that is high risk. PIPA limits collection to reasonable purposes and
generally requires consent or another legal authority. If the organization needs
a member list, it should seek a lawful data-sharing process or collect
information directly with proper notice and consent where appropriate.

### What if a person asks for their personal information?

The organization should search the records it controls, including service
providers and shared tools, then respond through the PIPA access-request
process. If a university or parent institution holds separate records outside
the organization's custody or control, say that clearly and direct the person to
that institution for those records.

### Are documents in Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Mailchimp, Airtable, or a voting platform under our control?

Often yes, if the account, workspace, contract, or system is used by or for the
organization. PIPA responsibility can extend to information controlled by the
organization even when another service provider stores it.

### Do member-register inspection rights override privacy duties?

No. Record-inspection rights and privacy duties need to be read together. A
society should only provide access to records in the manner allowed by the
governing statute, bylaws, and privacy rules. It should not casually publish a
member list or use inspection rights as a reason to ignore safeguards.

### What should we upload to Societyer?

Upload or link the current privacy policy if one exists. If the organization has
separate procedures, also keep the complaint process, access-request procedure,
retention schedule, privacy officer record, data-sharing agreements, and
member-data gap memo.

### How often should the policy be reviewed?

At least annually, and whenever the organization starts a new program, collects a
new type of personal information, changes systems, changes service providers, or
has a privacy complaint or breach.

## Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Small club with event signups

A small club collects names, emails, dietary restrictions, and payment details
for events.

Recommended handling:

- provide a short collection notice on the signup form;
- limit access to the event organizer and treasurer;
- keep payment records only as long as needed for accounting and legal reasons;
- delete dietary information after the event unless there is a clear reason to
  keep it;
- name a privacy contact; and
- keep a short privacy policy on file.

### Scenario 2: Student society without a university member list

A student society's bylaws say all eligible students are members, but the
university will not provide a list.

Recommended handling:

- document the gap and the university's response;
- maintain the member records the society can lawfully maintain;
- collect contact information directly from students with notice where
  appropriate;
- avoid claiming the institutional list is society-controlled if the society has
  no access, copy, or legal control;
- keep separate records for volunteers, directors, events, subscribers, and
  complaints; and
- get legal advice if the gap affects AGM notice, elections, referenda, or
  Societies Act member-register duties.

### Scenario 3: Referendum-only student data

A registrar gives a student society a dataset for a specific referendum or fee
process.

Recommended handling:

- use the data only for the authorized referendum or fee purpose;
- restrict access to the election/referendum team;
- do not import it into a general marketing or membership CRM;
- keep an audit trail of who accessed it;
- delete or archive it according to the specific legal, audit, and dispute
  requirements; and
- document the source, authority, purpose, and disposal date.

### Scenario 4: Larger society with casework

A society provides advocacy or support services and keeps intake notes,
complaints, referrals, and sensitive personal information.

Recommended handling:

- use a more detailed privacy policy and internal procedures;
- train staff and volunteers;
- restrict access by role;
- use secure systems rather than personal inboxes;
- document retention periods by record type;
- review service-provider contracts;
- maintain a breach-response process; and
- consider privacy review before new case-management tools or data sharing.

### Scenario 5: Member asks for the full member list

A member asks to inspect or copy the member register.

Recommended handling:

- check the governing statute, bylaws, and active inspection rules;
- verify whether the requester is entitled to inspect or receive a copy;
- disclose only the fields that may be included in the member register;
- apply any required purpose restrictions, fees, or request process;
- avoid disclosing unrelated personal information; and
- log the request and response.

## Societyer status wording

Future product copy should keep these ideas separate:

- "Policy/practices adopted" means the organization has a privacy baseline.
- "Privacy officer designated" means a responsible contact exists.
- "Policy evidence linked" means Societyer has a document attached.
- "Member-data gap documented" means the organization recorded why a full member
  list is unavailable or outside its control.

Suggested warning copy:

> No PIPA policy evidence linked. PIPA requires privacy policies/practices,
> a complaint process, and a privacy contact; a public registry document is not
> normally expected.

Suggested university/member-list copy:

> Member list not controlled by society. Document the institution's refusal or
> limits, maintain records the society can lawfully maintain, and continue PIPA
> compliance for personal information the society collects or controls.
